Worksheet 6 - Normally Open vs Normally Closed

1. Introduction

Push buttons or control contacts can be normally open (NO) or normally closed (NC). A NO contact only sends a signal when pressed, while an NC contact is active until it is pressed. This difference helps ensure safe and reliable control in industrial automation systems.

3. So What?

Why this matters

Understanding the difference between NO and NC is critical in maintenance. On I/O or mimic screens, you need to know whether an input should be on or off when the button is not pressed. For NO contacts, the input shows off until pressed; for NC, the input is on until pressed. If you don’t know this, you might think there’s a wiring fault when there isn’t.

Where NO and NC are used

Start/stop buttons, selector switches, float switches, and guards use NO or NC contacts. Safety devices are often NC so a broken wire triggers a fault (failsafe).

How to verify quickly

Use the HMI IO page and press the device: NO should change from off→on, NC from on→off. If it doesn’t, check wiring, device, or PLC input channel.

Key Takeaways

  • Normally Open (NO): off until pressed.
  • Normally Closed (NC): on until pressed.
  • Knowing expected I/O states helps spot faults quickly.
  • Misreading NO/NC can lead to unnecessary troubleshooting.

Questions & Answers

Question 1: What is the difference between a normally open (NO) and normally closed (NC) contact?

Correct Answer: B) An NO contact is off until pressed, while an NC contact is on until pressed.

Question 2: What does green text on the HMI input screen mean?

Correct Answer: C) Green means the input is currently on; white means it is off.

Question 3: How does Input 2 behave when not pressed?

Correct Answer: B) It stays on by default and turns off when the red button is pressed.

Question 4: What happens to Input 3 when it is pressed and released?

Correct Answer: D) It latches on when pressed and stays on until pressed again.

Question 5: Why is it important to understand NO and NC input states in maintenance?

Correct Answer: A) So you can correctly read input states and avoid misdiagnosing faults.
