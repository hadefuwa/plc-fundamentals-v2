Worksheet 9 - Temperature Sensor

1. Introduction

A temperature sensor tracks heat levels in real time to maintain safe and efficient system operation. Common in automation and process control, it helps prevent overheating or freezing. Engineers must understand these sensors to detect faults, ensure accurate readings, and avoid equipment damage or shutdowns.

3. So What?

Why this matters

Temperature sensors keep equipment safe by preventing overheating and detecting faults. We are using a PT100 probe connected to an evaluation unit that converts resistance to a 0–10 V signal for the PLC. The PLC reads a raw value (0–27,648) and scales it to a real temperature.

Scaling the reading

Raw values must be scaled to °C. Use the suggested scalar, then check against a known ambient (about 20–40 °C). A value near max can indicate a wiring fault.

Typical faults

Loose terminals, damaged probes, electrical noise, or drift in the transmitter can cause bad readings. Verify wiring first, then compare IO, HMI, and reality.

Key Takeaways

  • PT100 → evaluation unit → 0–10 V analogue input to PLC.
  • Raw value range 0–27,648 must be scaled to °C.
  • A reading near 32,000 indicates a fault (e.g., disconnected sensor).
  • Accurate scaling is essential (typical room 20–40 °C).

Questions & Answers

Question 1: What does a temperature sensor do in a PLC system?

Correct Answer: C) It tracks heat levels to help prevent overheating, freezing, or system faults.

Question 2: What type of temperature sensor is used in this worksheet?

Correct Answer: C) A PT100 RTD sensor.

Question 3: Why is a signal converter needed with an RTD sensor?

Correct Answer: B) Because the PLC can't read resistance directly—it needs a 0–10 V voltage signal.

Question 4: What does the PLC do with the analogue voltage from the sensor?

Correct Answer: D) It converts the voltage into a raw value, then multiplies it by a scalar to get temperature in °C.

Question 5: What might a raw value near 32,000 mean?

Correct Answer: C) It usually means a fault like a disconnected sensor, wrong input type, or wiring issue.
