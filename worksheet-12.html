Worksheet 12 - Motor Relay

1. Introduction

Relays are electrically operated switches that allow a PLC to control higher-power devices safely. The PLC energises a low-power coil, which activates internal contacts to switch outputs on or off. This worksheet helps you understand how relays isolate and control output devices like motors or solenoids.

3. So What?

Why this matters

In real systems, PLC outputs can’t always drive motors or solenoids directly. Relays bridge low-power PLC control and higher-power loads, and provide isolation. You’ll diagnose stuck contacts, faulty coils, or incorrect logic in the field.

How a relay works

The PLC energises a coil. That magnet pulls contacts together (or apart) to switch the load. You’ll often see a small relay LED when the coil is on.

Simple checks

If the output device won’t run, check: input command present, relay coil LED on, relay clicks, and output voltage at the load. Replace the relay if the coil works but contacts don’t switch.

Key Takeaways

  • Relays let PLCs safely switch higher-power devices.
  • PLC energises a coil; contacts switch the load.
  • Relays provide electrical isolation.
  • Status LEDs on relays/HMI help diagnosis.

Questions & Answers

Question 1: What is the main purpose of a relay in a PLC system?

Correct Answer: D) To safely control higher-power devices like motors or solenoids.

Question 2: How does the PLC activate a relay?

Correct Answer: C) By sending power to a coil inside the relay.

Question 3: What happens when the relay is energised?

Correct Answer: D) The relay closes internal contacts to switch the connected output on.

Question 4: Why can’t PLCs always control devices like motors directly?

Correct Answer: C) Because PLC outputs are low-power and need a relay to handle larger loads.

Question 5: What should you check if a relay does not switch as expected?

Correct Answer: B) Check for stuck contacts, faulty coils, incorrect logic, or wiring issues.
